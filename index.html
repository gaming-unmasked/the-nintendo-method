THE HIDDEN STORY

The Untold Secret of Nintendo’s Success

How Outsmarting Everyone—Including Ethics—Built a Gaming Empire

Ever wonder how Nintendo bounced back after losing to Sony’s PlayStation—then went on to dominate the console market? Grab your popcorn. The story’s juicier than you think. Read below.

Nintendo's Market Competition and Declining Share

How Nintendo Lost Market Share to Sony PlayStation

Since Sony launched the PlayStation in 1994, Nintendo has struggled to maintain its market share. At that time, the trend was to improve CPU and graphics performance, with game console manufacturers competing fiercely. Although Nintendo followed this trend, it was ultimately defeated by Sony, leading many game developers to switch their support from Nintendo to Sony. For example, million-selling games such as Dragon Quest and Final Fantasy transitioned from Nintendo consoles to the PlayStation.

The GameCube Struggle

In an attempt to regain its market position, Nintendo launched the GameCube in September 2001. However, the PlayStation 2, released in March 2000, featured superior specifications and a groundbreaking feature at the time: DVD playback. This further eroded Nintendo's market share as developers preferred the PlayStation 2, creating a vicious cycle of decline for Nintendo.

A Savior for Nintendo: A Revolutionary Proposal

In early 2002, Masao Kuwabara of Altron—a small but scrappy Japanese tech company—proposed a novel concept to Nintendo: a hybrid game machine with both handheld capabilities featuring a detachable controller and home console functionalities, covered under Patent No. 2768771.

Patent 2768771—Altron’s pride and joy—details a technology that could revolutionize the gaming experience. Thanks to Mr. Kuwabara, Nintendo had access to this patent, effectively getting a shortcut to cutting-edge innovation.

Nintendo instantly recognized this idea as pure gold. Why struggle to innovate when someone else has already done the heavy lifting? Despite recognizing the immense market potential of this innovative idea, Nintendo's management was unwilling to pay a fair license fee for Kuwabara's intellectual property. A masterstroke in cost-cutting brilliance!

Nintendo's Response and Alleged Intellectual Property Infringement

Nintendo President Hiroshi Yamauchi was upset that such an idea hadn't originated internally. He viewed the concept as a timely solution to Nintendo's challenges but rather than express gratitude, he stated, "We can make a product based on this idea ourselves, so we don't need to pay Kuwabara."
An inspiring display of business resourcefulness—repurposing someone else’s vision without the financial inconvenience of royalties.

Timeline of Events

  • 1998/11/30 – Mr. Kuwabara files Japanese Patent No. 2768771, outlining a hybrid game console concept that would later sound oddly familiar.
  • Early 2002 – Mr. Kuwabara presents his idea to Nintendo. A bold move—what could possibly go wrong?
  • May 2002 – At E3, Nintendo executives allegedly begin the “isolate the inventor” campaign. Club outings apparently become part of their IP strategy.
  • 2004 – Sony releases the PSP—without any of the magic from Kuwabara’s patent. Wonder why?
  • 2006 – Nintendo launches the Wii — a smash hit that redefines casual gaming. Motion controls capture a new demographic. Profits soar. But this momentum won’t last forever.
  • 2010 – The Wii’s success begins to fade. With the Wii U, Nintendo adds a handheld-style controller, but critics see it as a half-step rather than true innovation. Sales disappoint. Analysts speculate Nintendo might exit the console market and pivot to mobile gaming.
  • 2011–2015 – While pressure mounts, Nintendo makes no radical hardware moves. Internally, however, the company waits. Mr. Kuwabara’s patent — the legal wall guarding his hybrid console idea — is quietly nearing expiration. Patent expired in November 2016. Nintendo waited like true gentlemen—until it was legally safe to “innovate.”
  • November 30, 2016 – Kuwabara’s patent expires. What a convenient timing! 🛈
  • March 2017 – Nintendo unveils the Switch — four months after the patent expired. A coincidence? A revolutionary console-handheld hybrid, it's sleek, flexible — everything the Wii U wanted to be, but wasn’t. Critics hail it as genius. The gaming world embraces it. Nintendo’s fortunes rebound dramatically.
  • 2020s – The Switch becomes one of the best-selling consoles of all time. No one asks where the hybrid idea came from. But perhaps… they should.

The timeline reveals a pattern — but there's more beneath the surface.

Insider Trading and Shareholder Deception

Nintendo's game consoles are entirely dependent on Kuwabara's innovations, and Nintendo's sales soared whenever they leveraged those ideas. But as the Wii got older, sales dropped dramatically. The Wii U, released as a stopgap with only minor tweaks, failed to impress. Nintendo waited patiently until Kuwabara's patent expired, and then—voilà!—the Switch was born, sending sales through the roof. Looking at the trend, one might notice: Nintendo's profits spike right after each 'inspired' release, only to taper off when the magic runs out.

Cheating the Shareholder

The real issue? Nintendo doesn't innovate —not in the way shareholders think. The upcoming “Switch 2” uses an Nvidia chip and is essentially a sleeker version of the original. Meanwhile, shareholders are kept blissfully unaware that these flagship products are, in fact, secondhand brilliance. The illusion of endless innovation is carefully maintained, while the true inventors remain invisible.

Insider Trading

The Yamauchi family heirs—including Yamauchi Jr.—understand the uncomfortable truth: Nintendo’s success is built on borrowed innovation. Once Kuwabara's ideas run out, so will the magic. So what did they do? Sold their stock, naturally—right before the public realized the innovation pipeline had dried up. A classic case of exit early, profit greatly, while ordinary shareholders are left holding the joystick in the dark.

How Nintendo Allegedly Stole and Adapted Mr. Kuwabara's Innovations

  • Nintendo Wii:
    Developed after President Yamauchi realized the controller's critical role in gaming, following Mr. Kuwabara's concept. Yamauchi directed Nintendo of America to collect all available controllers in the U.S., led to the creation of an interactive controller that translated movements into gameplay—a direct adaptation of Mr. Kuwabara's idea emphasizing the controller's importance.
    A phenomenal example of turning borrowed brilliance into billion-dollar sales!
  • Nintendo Wii U:
    Incorporated a monitor into the controller itself, directly mirroring Mr. Kuwabara's vision of integrating display and control functions.
    They say imitation is the sincerest form of flattery.
  • Nintendo Switch:
    Fully embodies Mr. Kuwabara's concept with its detachable controllers and the main unit's dual functionality, connecting to a home TV for traditional gaming or serving as a portable unit when attached to the controllers.
    Refine, rebrand, and reap the rewards—Nintendo’s classic strategy!
“Did you know Nintendo released the Switch four months after the patent expired? Coincidence? We think not.”

Use of Patented Features in TV Ads

Nintendo used some of Kuwabara's patented features in a TV ad, showing players using individual handheld monitors to enjoy games from their own perspectives instead of sharing a TV screen. Concerned about the similarity to the patented features, Nintendo quickly withdrew the ad.
What a genius tactic: advertise the innovation, then quietly erase the evidence.

Historical Background: Nintendo's Copycat Practices and Disregard for Intellectual Property

Historically, Nintendo has often copied successful concepts from other companies, such as the Lego knockoff "N&B Blocks" in the 1960s and the bootleg "Space Invaders" called "Space Fever" in 1979.
They've been mastering the art of "creative borrowing" for decades.

Before entering the video game industry, Nintendo wasn't exactly picky about its revenue streams. The company once operated love hotels in the 1960s, and their earlier business involved supplying hanafuda cards to the yakuza. In other words, ethical concerns rarely got in the way of their hustle.

President Yamauchi's Public Stance on Intellectual Property:

President Yamauchi's dismissive stance on intellectual property was evident in his infamous statement on the TV show 'Reportage Nippon,' where he asserted, 'There are no patents on the way to play.' and ‘For the development of the amusement industry, it is important that such things are made public and shared.’
What a philanthropic vision—other people’s ideas should be shared… especially when you can profit from them!

How Mr. Kuwabara’s “Brilliant Idea” Became Nintendo’s Best-Kept Secret

While the rest of the gaming world scrambled to innovate, Nintendo got a great boost. Enter Mr. Kuwabara, who casually handed Nintendo a patented technology so obscure (at the time) and secret that not even Sony had heard of it. Imagine that—while Sony was busy making consoles, Nintendo was quietly holding the winning card under the table.
Of course, discretion is key. Nintendo wisely decided to keep this patent under wraps, making sure the competition stayed blissfully unaware. Why let Sony in on the secret when you can just build the Wii, Wii U, and Switch with it? Because nothing screams “innovation” like borrowing your neighbor’s homework and pretending you wrote it yourself.

Fraudulent Acts: Misleading Mr. Kuwabara

President Yamauchi intended to leverage Mr. Kuwabara's idea without compensating him but feared the possibility of the idea being sold to Sony, who was also developing a portable game console at that time.
To prevent this, Nintendo misled Mr. Kuwabara, convincing him that his idea lacked market potential and dissuading him from selling it to other competitors.
Behind closed doors, Nintendo's strategy meetings may well have been titled ‘How to Borrow Genius Without Paying for It.’
Step one: flatter the inventor just enough to keep him close.
Step two: gently crush his confidence so he doesn't realize he’s sitting on gold.
It’s not deception—it’s visionary stakeholder management!
An elite maneuver—outsmart the inventor to secure a monopoly.

Intellectual Property Infringement and Unfair Competition

Mr. Kuwabara's innovative concepts significantly influenced Nintendo's development strategies. Upon understanding that merely enhancing CPU and graphics performance increased development costs and complexity, President Yamauchi shifted focus, as inspired by Mr. Kuwabara's insights.
This led to the strategic collection of all controllers across the U.S., facilitating the development of the Wii with its groundbreaking controller design, followed by the Nintendo Switch.
Why compete on CPU and graphics performance when you can win with stolen simplicity? The Switch was introduced after the expiration of Mr. Kuwabara's patent, enabling Nintendo to capitalize on his ideas without acknowledgment or compensation.
Timing is everything. Wait out the patent and then roll out a billion-dollar product—genius!

Abuse of Trust: Anti-Competitive Behavior at E3

During major industry events like E3, Nintendo representatives actively worked to prevent Mr. Kuwabara from meeting with Sony executives. At the E3 event held on May 22, 2002, Nintendo's general manager Hisashi Yuasa noticed Mr. Kuwabara's presence and voiced concerns to Katsuhito Yamauchi, commonly known as "Yamauchi Jr."
Yamauchi Jr.’s clever tactic of whisking Mr. Kuwabara away to a club in Little Tokyo? A masterstroke in friendly sabotage.
This tactic was repeatedly employed at subsequent game shows, where either General Manager Yuasa or Deputy General Manager Ishizuka would escort Mr. Kuwabara to meals or social outings, thus isolating him from other industry players.
They really thought of everything—Networking prevention as corporate strategy!

Anti-competitive Conduct: Impact on Sony PSP

The Sony PSP, launched in 2004, could have incorporated similar innovative technology if Sony had been aware of Mr. Kuwabara's ideas.
Thanks to Nintendo’s protective strategy, they kept that tech exclusively to themselves. So clever, it almost hurts.
